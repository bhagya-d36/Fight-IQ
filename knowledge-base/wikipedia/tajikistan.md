# Tajikistan

Tajikistan, officially the Republic of Tajikistan, is a landlocked country located in Central Asia. Dushanbe is the capital and largest city of the country, with a population of over 1.2 million. Tajikistan borders Afghanistan to the south, Uzbekistan to the west, Kyrgyzstan to the north, and China to the east; it is narrowly separated from Pakistan by Afghanistan's Wakhan Corridor. Its total population exceeds 11 million.
The territory was previously home to cultures of the Neolithic and the Bronze Age, including the Oxus civilisation in the west. Parts of the country were part of the Sogdian and Bactrian civilisations, and were ruled by the Achaemenids, Alexander the Great, the Greco‑Bactrians, the Kushans, the Kidarites, the Hephthalites, the First Turkic Khaganate, the Umayyad and Abbasid Caliphates, the Samanid Empire, the Kara‑Khanids, the Seljuks, the Khwarazmians, the Mongols, the Timurids, and the Khanate of Bukhara. The region was later conquered by the Russian Empire, before becoming part of the Soviet Union. Within the Soviet Union, the country's borders were drawn when it was part of Uzbekistan as an autonomous republic, before becoming a constituent republic of the Soviet Union on 5 December 1929.
On 9 September 1991, Tajikistan declared itself an independent sovereign state as the Soviet Union was disintegrating. A civil war was fought after independence, lasting from May 1992 to June 1997. Since the end of the war, newly established political stability and foreign aid have allowed the country's economy to grow. The country has been led since 1994 by Emomali Rahmon, who heads an authoritarian regime and whose human rights record has been criticised.
Tajikistan is a presidential republic consisting of four provinces. Tajiks form the ethnic majority in the country, and their national language is Tajik. Russian is used as the official inter‑ethnic language. While the state is constitutionally secular, Islam is nominally adhered to by 97.5% of the population. In the Gorno-Badakhshan oblast, there is considerable linguistic diversity, with languages such as Rushani, Shughni, Ishkashimi, Wakhi, and Tajik being spoken. Mountains cover more than 90% of the country. It is a developing country with a transitional economy that is dependent on remittances and on the production of aluminium and cotton. Tajikistan is a member of the United Nations, CIS, OSCE, OIC, ECO, SCO, and CSTO, and is a NATO PfP partner.

## Etymology

The term 'Tajik' ultimately derives from the Middle Persian Tāzīk, the Turkic rendition of the Arabic ethnonym Ṭayyi’, denoting a Qahtanite Arab tribe that migrated to the Transoxiana region of Central Asia in the 7th century AD. Before 1991, Tajikistan appeared in English as Tadjikistan or Tadzhikistan. This is owing to a transliteration from the Russian: "Таджикистан". In Russian, there is no single letter 'j' to represent the phoneme /d͡ʒ/; instead, дж (transliterated as dzh) is used. Tadzhikistan is an alternative spelling and is found in English‑language literature derived from Russian sources.

## History

Cultures in the region have been dated back to at least the fourth millennium BC, including the Bronze Age Bactria–Margiana Archaeological Complex, the Andronovo culture and the pro-urban site of Sarazm, a UNESCO World Heritage Site.
The earliest recorded history of the region dates back to about 500 BC when most, if not all, of Tajikistan was a part of the Achaemenid Empire. Some authors have suggested that in the seventh and sixth centuries BC parts of Tajikistan, including territories in the Zeravshan Valley, formed part of the Kambojas before they became part of the Achaemenid Empire. After the region's conquest by Alexander the Great it became part of the Greco-Bactrian Kingdom, a successor state of Alexander's empire. Northern Tajikistan (the cities of Khujand and Panjakent) was a part of Sogdiana, a collection of city-states which were overrun by Scytho-Siberians and Yuezhi nomadic tribes around 150 BC. The Silk Road passed through the region and following the expedition of Chinese explorer Zhang Qian during the reign of Wudi (141 BC – 87 BC) commercial relations between Han Empire and Sogdiana flourished. Sogdians played a role in facilitating trade and worked in other capacities, as farmers, carpetweavers, glassmakers, and woodcarvers.
The Kushan Empire, a collection of Yuezhi tribes, took control of the region in the first century AD and ruled until the fourth century AD during which time Buddhism, Nestorian Christianity, Zoroastrianism, and Manichaeism were practised in the region. Later the Hephthalite Empire, a collection of nomadic tribes, moved into the region, and the Arabs propagated Islam in the eighth century.

### Samanid Empire

Four brothers – Nuh, Ahmad, Yahya, and Ilyas – founded the Samanid Empire. Each of them ruled territory under Abbasid suzerainty. In 892, Ismail Samani (892–907) united the Samanid state under a single ruler, thereby putting an end to the feudal system that had previously prevailed. It was under his rule that the Samanids became independent of Abbasid authority. The Samanid Empire (819–999) restored Persian control of the region and expanded the cities of Samarkand and Bukhara, which became the cultural centres of the Iranian world; the region was known as Khorasan. The empire was centred on Khorasan and Transoxiana, and at its greatest extent encompassed Afghanistan, parts of Iran, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan, Uzbekistan, Kyrgyzstan, parts of Kazakhstan, and Pakistan.
The Samanids revived Persian culture by patronising Rudaki, Bal'ami, and Daqiqi. The Samanids actively promoted Sunni Islam and suppressed Ismaili Shi'ism, but were more tolerant of Twelver Shi'ism. Islamic architecture and Perso‑Islamic culture were spread deep into the heartlands of Central Asia by the Samanids. Following the first complete translation of the Qur'an into Persian in the 9th century, populations under the Samanid Empire began to accept Islam in significant numbers.
The Karluk Turkic Kara-Khanid Khanate conquered Transoxania (which corresponded approximately to what would later become Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, southern Kyrgyzstan, and south‑west Kazakhstan) and ruled there from 999 to 1211. Their arrival in Transoxania marked a definitive shift from Iranian to Turkic predominance in Central Asia. Over time, the Kara‑Khanids became assimilated into the Perso‑Arab Muslim culture of the region.
The region was later ruled by the Ghurid and Kart dynasties, both of Tajik origin. While the Kart dynasty ruled in present‑day Afghanistan, Muhammad of Ghor, a ruler of the Ghurid dynasty, conquered large parts of India.
In the 13th century, the Mongol Empire swept through Central Asia, invaded the Khwarezmian Empire, and sacked its cities, looting and massacring their inhabitants. The Turco‑Mongol conqueror Tamerlane founded the Timurid Empire, becoming the first ruler of the Timurid dynasty in what later became Tajikistan and Central Asia.

### Bukharan rule

The territory that later became Tajikistan came under the rule of the Khanate of Bukhara during the 16th century. Following the khanate's collapse in the 18th century, the region fell under the control of the Emirate of Bukhara and the Khanate of Kokand. The Emirate of Bukhara remained intact until the 20th century.

### Imperial Russia

During the 19th century, the Russian Empire began to conquer parts of the region. Russian Imperialism led to the Russian Empire's conquest of Central Asia during the 19th century's Imperial Era. Between 1864 and 1885, Russia gradually took control of the entire territory of Russian Turkestan, the Tajikistan portion of which had been controlled by the Emirate of Bukhara and Khanate of Kokand. Russia was interested in gaining access to a supply of cotton and in the 1870s attempted to switch cultivation in the region from grain to cotton (a strategy later copied and expanded by the Soviets).
During the 19th century, the Jadidists established themselves as an Islamic social movement throughout the region. While the Jadidists were pro-modernisation and not necessarily anti-Russian, the Russians viewed the movement as a threat because the Russian Empire was predominantly Christian. Russian troops were required to restore order during uprisings against the Khanate of Kokand between 1910 and 1913. Further violence occurred in July 1916 when demonstrators attacked Russian soldiers in Khujand over the threat of forced conscription during World War I. While Russian troops brought Khujand back under control, clashes continued throughout the year in various locations in Tajikistan.

### Soviet period

After the Russian Revolution of 1917, guerrillas throughout Central Asia, known as basmachi, waged a war against Bolshevik armies in an attempt to maintain independence. The Bolsheviks prevailed after a four‑year war, during which mosques and villages were burned down and the population was suppressed.
Between 1928 and 1941, Soviet authorities began an anti‑religious campaign of secularisation. Practising Christianity, Islam, or Judaism was discouraged and repressed; under Soviet anti-religious legislation, several churches, mosques, and synagogues were closed. As a consequence of the conflict and Soviet agricultural policies, Central Asia, including Tajikistan, suffered a famine that claimed many lives.
Tajikistan was first created in 1924 as the Tajik Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic (abbreviated as the Tajik ASSR), an autonomous republic within the Uzbek Soviet Socialist Republic. Despite their historical status as Tajik cities and their largely Tajik populations, none of the regional urban centres – such as Bukhara, Samarkand, Tirmiz, Qarshi, and Khojand – were included in Tajikistan. Instead, the Tajikistani capital was established in Dushanbe, a mountain town with approximately 1,000 residents.
A specific Persian dialect, with vocabulary and pronunciation distinct from formal written Persian, was deliberately made the national language of Tajikistan by the Soviets and renamed 'Tajiki' in order to forcibly separate it from the common variant of Persian spoken from Istanbul to Calcutta. The Tajiks were also excluded from the enormous global world of Persian literature, both past and present, by the creation of a new phonetic alphabet – first Latin‑based in 1928 and later Cyrillic in 1940. With Persian confined to Tajikistan and replaced by Russian as Central Asia's common language, the unity of the Persianate world was broken, steering Soviet and post‑Soviet Central Asia on to a developmental path distinct from that of Iran and Afghanistan. In 1929, the Tajik ASSR was elevated to its own full union republic, the Tajik Soviet Socialist Republic. The Sughd Region was added to Tajikistan in the same year.
Between 1927 and 1934, collectivisation of agriculture and an expansion of cotton production took place, especially in the southern region. Soviet collectivization policy brought violence against farmers and peasants, classified as anti-Soviet categories of "enemies of the people", and forced resettlement occurred throughout Tajikistan. Consequently, some peasants fought collectivisation and revived the Basmachi movement. Some industrial development occurred during this time along with the expansion of irrigation infrastructure.

Two rounds of Stalin's purges (1927–1934 and 1937–1938) resulted in the expulsion of nearly 10,000 people from all levels of the Communist Party of Tajikistan. Ethnic Russians were sent in to replace those expelled and subsequently Russians dominated party positions at all levels, including the top position of first secretary. Between 1926 and 1959 the proportion of Russians among Tajikistan's population grew from less than 1% to 13%. Bobojon Ghafurov, First Secretary of the Communist Party of Tajikistan from 1946 to 1956, was the only Tajik politician of significance outside of the republic during the Soviet Era.
Tajiks began to be conscripted into the Red Army in 1939, and during the Second World War around 260,000 Tajik citizens fought against Nazi Germany, Finland, and the Empire of Japan. Between 60,000 (4%) and 120,000 (8%) of Tajikistan's 1,530,000 citizens were killed during the war.
Following the war and the end of Stalin's rule, attempts were made to further expand the agriculture and industry of Tajikistan. During 1957–58, Nikita Khrushchev's Virgin Lands Campaign focused attention on Tajikistan, where living conditions, education, and industry lagged behind those of the other Soviet republics. In the 1980s, Tajikistan had the lowest household saving rate in the USSR, the lowest percentage of households in the two top per capita income groups, and the lowest rate of university graduates per 1,000 people.
By the 1980s, Tajik nationalists were calling for increased rights. Real disturbances did not occur within the republic until 1990. The following year, the Soviet Union collapsed, and Tajikistan declared its independence on 9 September 1991, a day which is celebrated as the country's Independence Day.

### Independence

In February 1990, riots and strikes in Dushanbe and other cities began because of the difficult socio‑economic situation, a shortage of housing, and youth unemployment. The nationalist and democratic opposition, along with supporters of independence, joined the strikes and began to demand the independence of the republic and democratic reforms. Islamists also began to hold strikes to demand respect for their rights and independence. The Soviet leadership introduced Internal Troops in Dushanbe to put an end to the unrest.

Following independence, the nation fell into civil war among factions distinguished by clan loyalties. Regional groups from the Gharm and Gorno-Badakhshan regions of Tajikistan, led by a combination of liberal democratic reformers and Islamists, who eventually became the United Tajik Opposition, rose up against the newly formed government of President Rahmon Nabiyev, which was dominated by people from the Khujand and Kulob regions.
The war lasted until 1997. More than 500,000 residents fled during this time because of persecution and increased poverty, seeking better economic opportunities in the West or in other former Soviet republics. The estimated number of dead exceeded 100,000. Around 1.2 million people were refugees, both inside and outside the country.
Emomali Rahmon came to power in the early part of this conflict in 1992, after Nabiyev was forced at gunpoint on 7 September 1992 to resign from office. Rahmon defeated the former prime minister Abdumalik Abdullajanov in a presidential election in November with 58% of the vote.
In 1997, a ceasefire was reached between Rahmon and opposition parties under the guidance of Gerd D. Merrem, Special Representative to the Secretary General, a result praised as a successful United Nations peacekeeping initiative. The ceasefire guaranteed 30% of ministerial positions would go to the opposition. Elections were held in 1999 and were criticised by opposition parties and foreign observers as unfair; Rahmon was re-elected with 98% of the vote. Elections in 2006 were again won by Rahmon (with 79% of the vote) and he began his third term in office. Opposition parties boycotted the 2006 election and the Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe (OSCE) criticised it, while observers from the Commonwealth of Independent States claimed the elections were legal and transparent.
Rahmon's administration came under further criticism from OSCE in October 2010 for its censorship and repression of the media. OSCE claimed that the Tajik Government censored Tajik and foreign websites and instituted tax inspections on independent printing houses that led to the cessation of printing activities for a number of independent newspapers.
Russian border troops were stationed along the Tajik–Afghan border until summer 2005. Since the September 11, 2001 attacks, French troops have been stationed at Dushanbe Airport in support of air operations of NATO's International Security Assistance Force in Afghanistan. United States Army and Marine Corps personnel periodically visit Tajikistan to conduct joint training missions of up to several weeks' duration. The Government of India rebuilt the Ayni Air Base, a military airport located 15 km southwest of Dushanbe, at a cost of $70 million, completing the repairs in September 2010. It is the main base of the Tajikistan Air Force. There have been talks with Russia concerning use of the Ayni facility, and Russia continues to maintain a base on the outskirts of Dushanbe.
In 2010, there were concerns among Tajik officials that Islamic militarism in the east of the country was on the rise following the escape of 25 militants from a Tajik prison in August, an ambush that killed 28 Tajik soldiers in the Rasht Valley in September, and another ambush in the valley in October that killed 30 soldiers, followed by fighting outside Gharm that left three militants dead. The country's Interior Ministry insisted that the central government maintained full control over the country's east, and the military operation in the Rasht Valley was concluded in November 2010.
Fighting erupted again, this time in and around Gorno-Badakhshan, in July 2012. In 2015, Russia sent more troops to Tajikistan.
In May 2015, Tajikistan's national security underwent a setback when Colonel Gulmurod Khalimov, commander of the special-purpose police unit (OMON) of the Interior Ministry, defected to the Islamic State. Khalimov was allegedly killed on 8 September 2017 during a Russian airstrike near Deir ez-Zor, Syria, although Tajikistan authorities express doubts whether he has died.
In 2021, following the Fall of Kabul, Tajikistan allegedly got involved in the Panjshir conflict against the Taliban on the side of the National Resistance Front of Afghanistan.
In September 2022 armed clashes, including the use of artillery, erupted along most of the border between Kyrgyzstan and Tajikistan.

## Politics

After independence, Tajikistan was plunged into a civil war. Factions were supported by foreign countries including Afghanistan, Iran, Pakistan, Uzbekistan and Russia. Russia and Iran focused on keeping peace in the warring nation to decrease the chances of U.S. or Turkish involvement. Russia backed the pro-government faction and deployed troops from the Commonwealth of Independent States to guard the Tajikistan-Afghan border. All but 25,000 of the more than 400,000 ethnic Russians, who were mostly employed in industry, fled to Russia. By 1997, the war had ended after a peace agreement between the government and the Islamist-led opposition, and a central government began to take form, with peaceful elections in 1999.
"Longtime observers of Tajikistan often characterize the country as profoundly averse to risk and skeptical of promises of reform, a political passivity they trace to the country's ruinous civil war," Ilan Greenberg wrote in a news article in The New York Times before the country's November 2006 presidential election.

The parliamentary elections of 2005 aroused accusations from opposition parties and international observers that President Emomali Rahmon corruptly manipulated the election process and unemployment. Elections in February 2010 saw the ruling PDPT lose four seats in Parliament, yet still maintain a majority. The Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe election observers said the 2010 polling "failed to meet many key OSCE commitments" and that "these elections failed on many basic democratic standards." The government insisted that only minor violations had occurred, which would not affect the will of the Tajik people.
The Tajik government has reportedly clamped down on facial hair as part of a crackdown on Islamic influence and due to its perceived associations with Islamic extremism, which is evident in bordering Afghanistan.

The presidential election held on 6 November 2006 was boycotted by "mainline" opposition parties, including the 23,000-member Islamic Renaissance Party. Four remaining opponents "all but endorsed the incumbent", Rahmon.
Freedom of the press is officially guaranteed by the government, and independent press outlets remain restricted, as does an amount of web content. According to the Institute for War & Peace Reporting, access to local and foreign websites is blocked, and journalists are sometimes obstructed from reporting on some events. In practice, no public criticism of the regime is tolerated and all direct protest is suppressed and denied coverage in the local media.
In the 2020 Democracy Index by the Economist Intelligence Unit, Tajikistan was ranked 160th, after Saudi Arabia, while receiving the designation of "authoritarian regime".
In July 2019, UN ambassadors of 37 countries, including Tajikistan, signed a joint letter to UNHRC defending China's treatment of Uyghurs in the Xinjiang region.

In October 2020, President Emomali Rahmon was re-elected for another seven-year term with 90% of the vote, following a largely ceremonial election.
In April 2021, a conflict over water with Kyrgyzstan escalated into one of the border clashes between the two countries since independence.
In July 2021, Tajikistan appealed to members of a Russian-led Collective Security Treaty Organization (CSTO) of ex-Soviet states for help in dealing with security challenges emerging from neighbouring Afghanistan. The safety concerns emerged as foreign troops such as the US and British army exited the country, causing over 1,000 Afghan civilians and servicemen to flee to neighbouring Tajikistan after Taliban insurgents took control of parts of Afghanistan.

Tajikistan is landlocked and is the smallest nation in Central Asia by area. It lies mostly between latitudes 36° and 41° N, and longitudes 67° and 75° E. It is covered by mountains of the Pamir range, and most of the country is over 3,000 metres (9,800 ft) above sea level. The areas of lower land are in the north (part of the Fergana Valley) and in the southern valleys of the Kofarnihon and Vakhsh rivers, which form the Amu Darya. Dushanbe is located on the southern slopes above the Kofarnihon valley.

The Amu Darya and Panj rivers mark the border with Afghanistan, and the glaciers in Tajikistan's mountains are the source of runoff for the Aral Sea. There are over 900 rivers in Tajikistan longer than 10 kilometres.

Tajikistan consists of four administrative divisions at the first level. These are the provinces (viloyat) of Sughd and Khatlon, the autonomous province of Gorno-Badakhshan (abbreviated as GBAO), and the Regions of Republican Subordination (RRP – Raiony Respublikanskogo Podchineniya in transliteration from Russian; Tajik: Ноҳияҳои тобеи ҷумҳурӣ, romanized: Nohiyahoi tobei jumhurii). The capital city, Dushanbe, is administered separately as a city of republican subordination.
Each region is divided into districts (Tajik: Ноҳия, romanized: nohiya or raion). As of 2024, there are 47 districts in Tajikistan (excluding the 4 city districts within Dushanbe). The districts are further subdivided into jamoats (village-level self-governing units), which are then divided into villages (qyshloqs). As of 2024, there are approximately 406 jamoats.

### Biodiversity

Tajikistan contains five terrestrial ecoregions: Alai-Western Tian Shan steppe, Gissaro-Alai open woodlands, Pamir alpine desert and tundra, Badghyz and Karabil semi-desert, and Paropamisus xeric woodlands.

## Economy

Tajikistan's economy has experienced significant growth in recent years, with real GDP expanding by 8.4% in 2024 and maintaining a growth rate of 8.4% in 2025, reaching a nominal GDP of 176.9 billion somoni. Growth in the first half of 2025 averaged 8.1%. The World Bank projects growth to moderate to 6.2% in 2026 and 4.7% in 2027, while the IMF forecasts 6.0% for 2026. The government's own target for 2026 remains 8.5%.
In 2024, Tajikistan remained one of the world's most remittance-dependent economies, with remittances accounting for approximately 49% of GDP, up from 39% in 2023. The World Bank estimated remittance inflows at $5.8 billion in 2024, representing a 27% increase. In the first quarter of 2025, remittances surged by 62.9% in dollar terms compared to the same period in 2024, driven primarily by higher wages for migrant workers in Russia. However, the World Bank has warned that a downturn in the Russian economy could lead to returning migrants facing limited economic prospects at home.
Inflation has declined to historic lows, averaging 3.6% in 2024 and 3.5% in 2025—the lowest since independence. Food prices rose by 4.5%, services by 5.7%, and non-food goods by 1.5% in 2025. The National Bank of Tajikistan targets an inflation range of 3–7%, while the World Bank projects inflation at 4.5% for 2026.
Poverty has declined substantially, with the national poverty rate falling from 56% in 2010 to approximately 20% in 2024. The middle class grew from 8% to 33% over the same period. Under the lower-middle-income country poverty line ($4.20 per day in 2017 PPP), 16.9% of the population lived in poverty in 2024. Despite these gains, the World Bank notes that poverty reduction has been driven primarily by wage growth and remittances rather than domestic job creation. Labour force participation remains low at only 40%—the lowest in Central Asia—with female participation at just 21%.

The primary sources of income in Tajikistan remain aluminium production, cotton growing, and remittances from migrant workers. The state-owned Tajik Aluminum Company (TadAZ) produced 83,000 tonnes of aluminium in 2024, representing a 24% increase, with a target of 180,000 tonnes for 2025. Aluminium exports reached 71,110 tonnes in 2024, a 36.5% increase, though the sector accounts for only 1.33% of industrial output.
Cotton production, however, has declined sharply from 404,700 tonnes in 2022 to 253,200 tonnes in 2024. Fibre exports totalled 100,500 tonnes worth $170.1 million in 2024, with Iran receiving 67.9%, Turkey 15%, and China 8.4%. Cotton no longer accounts for 60% of agricultural output as previously reported, reflecting a significant restructuring of the agricultural sector.
Gold production grew by 18.8% in 2024, though exports of precious metals fell by 69.6% due to reduced gold shipments.
Tajikistan's hydropower potential remains a key focus, with the Rogun Dam project progressing. The World Bank approved $350 million in financing in late 2024, and the OPEC Fund provided a $25 million loan for construction. Once completed, the 335-metre dam will be the tallest in the world, with a capacity of 3,780 MW. The Nurek Dam, currently the second highest dam in the world, remains operational. Other hydroelectric projects include the Sangtuda-1 and Sangtuda-2 stations. Russia's RAO UES completed Sangtuda-1 (670 MW capacity) which commenced operations in January 2008. Iran has been involved in Sangtuda-2, and Chinese company SinoHydro has worked on the Zerafshan project.
The planned CASA-1000 project aims to transmit 1,000 MW of surplus electricity from Tajikistan to Pakistan via Afghanistan. The 750 km transmission line is being developed as a public-private partnership with support from the World Bank, IFC, ADB, and IDB, with an estimated cost of approximately US$865 million. Other energy resources include coal deposits and smaller reserves of natural gas and petroleum.
The unemployment rate remains a subject of debate, with official registered unemployment at approximately 50,900 people (June 2025), while international estimates from the World Bank place the rate at approximately 11.6% (2024). This discrepancy reflects the large informal economy and underemployment not captured in official figures.
Drug trafficking remains an illegal source of income, with Tajikistan serving as a transit country for Afghan narcotics bound for Russian and European markets. The UNODC continues to work with Tajik authorities to strengthen border controls, provide training, and establish joint interdiction teams. The Tajikistani Drug Control Agency has been established with international support. However, according to some experts, drug money has contributed to corruption at various levels of government. Tajikistan ranks among the leading countries globally for heroin and raw opium confiscations, though recent figures have not been publicly updated from earlier records.
China has become a significant economic partner under the Belt and Road Initiative, with total Chinese investment in Tajikistan reaching approximately $4.1 billion between 2007 and 2023. Over 700 Chinese companies operate in the country, spanning infrastructure, mining, energy, and manufacturing sectors.
The World Bank's 2025 assessment notes that while Tajikistan has made significant progress in economic growth and poverty reduction, the economy remains vulnerable to external shocks, particularly fluctuations in remittance flows from Russia and changes in global commodity prices. Diversification away from reliance on remittances, aluminium, and cotton remains a key policy challenge.

## Transportation

As a landlocked country, Tajikistan has no ports, and the majority of transportation is via road, air, and rail. In 2025, the country had an ambitious agenda to modernize its transport sector, focusing on infrastructure upgrades and stronger international connections. As of 2026, the country is implementing 11 investment projects in the road and transport sector with a total value of USD 1.193 billion. Under the State Budget for 2025, nearly 3 billion somoni ($274.35 million) was allocated to the transport and communications sector.
The government has pursued agreements with Iran, Afghanistan, and Pakistan to gain access to ports and establish regional transit corridors. In July 2026, Iran, Afghanistan, and Tajikistan signed a trilateral transit corridor agreement to activate a new road transit route. Afghanistan, Iran, and Turkey agreed to jointly construct a railway between Herat and Mazar-i-Sharif in 2025. Tajikistan has also been exploring participation in the China–Kyrgyzstan–Uzbekistan railway project.
In 2025, a total of 154.8 million tons of cargo were transported by all modes of transport, which is 15.2 percent more compared to 2024. In 2025, road transport carried 147.3 million tons of cargo and 1,129.5 million passengers.

### Road

Road transport continues to dominate Tajikistan's transport system, accounting for more than 90% of passenger transportation and over 80% of domestic freight. As of 2025, the total length of roads in the country is approximately 27,800 kilometres. In 2025, through state investment projects, 236 kilometers of roads and other transport infrastructure facilities were built and put into operation, with an investment of 5.4 billion somoni (about $580 million). Currently, there are 16 transport sector projects under construction nationwide, with a total investment of about 11.3 billion somoni (about $1.22 billion).

The construction of the Rogun reservoir affected the Obigarm–Nurobod section of the M41 highway. The 75-kilometer Rogun–Obigarm–Nurobod International Highway was opened in August 2025, bypassing the Rogun reservoir. The project includes three tunnels—"35th Anniversary of Independence" (2,605 m), "Vahdat" (1,700 m), and "Oriyono" (1,600 m)—which shorten the route by 24 km, as well as 14 bridges spanning approximately 1,150 meters. This route connects the capital with the administrative centers of the Nurobod, Rasht, Sangvor, Tojikobod and Lakhsh districts, expanding communication in all directions, including trade turnover between Tajikistan and Kyrgyzstan and regional transit. In June 2025, the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank approved a $2.04 million grant—its first under a Project-Specific Window—to support restoration of the 75-kilometer stretch.
The launch of the Dangara–Guliston Green Corridor project (49 km) began in March 2025, upgrading the road from two to four lanes and adding bike lanes, sidewalks, lighting, crossings, and electric vehicle charging stations. Financing comes from a mixture of international sources, including a European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD) loan of up to €38 million and an Asian Development Bank (ADB) grant of up to $86.7 million, with completion expected by 2030.
The Dushanbe–Chanak highway (358 km), connecting the capital to the Uzbekistan border, continues to undergo maintenance and reconstruction. As of August 2025, approximately 347,000 tons of asphalt had been used in its repair since 2012. The route includes three tunnels—"Maikhura" (897 m), "Istiklol" (4,998 m), and "Shakhristan" (5,253 m)—as well as 38 bridges and 24 avalanche galleries.
The Qalaikhumb–Vanj–Rushan section of the Dushanbe–Kulob–Khorog–Kulma highway was completed in 2025, funded by a $230 million grant from the Chinese government. President Emomali Rahmon inaugurated a section of the Kulob–Khorog–Kulma–China highway in 2025.
In February 2025, Tajikistan signed an agreement with China's Zhejiang Communications Construction Group to build Central Asia's longest road bridge over the Surkhab River. The bridge will span 920 meters (1,500 meters including access roads) and is expected to be completed within four years at a cost of nearly $60 million. The bridge will be the largest road bridge in the republic in terms of design and style.
Tajikistan and China have agreed to build 93 km of roads and two tunnels (total length 5.2 km) as part of a broader infrastructure partnership. The project includes five avalanche corridors totaling 602 meters, 15 bridges totaling 633 meters, 21,850 meters of protective walls, and 151 artificial structures. In September 2025, the governments of Tajikistan and China signed an agreement on international road transportation, along with seven other supplementary instruments in Beijing, encompassing various domains of bilateral collaboration.
China has resumed construction of a strategic 109-kilometer section of the Dushanbe–Kulma Highway near the Afghan border after several months of suspension, following new security guarantees provided by the Tajik government. Construction resumed in April 2026 after Tajik authorities introduced enhanced security measures to protect Chinese engineers and workers. The project had been suspended in late 2025 following a series of violent attacks on Chinese nationals.
In December 2025, Tajikistan and Kuwaiti consulting company NARCO signed an agreement for the rehabilitation of the 162-kilometer Ayni–Kuhistoni Mastchoh road in Sughd province. The project includes road surface modernization, widening of the roadway, the construction of 27 bridges and 405 culverts, and the updating of engineering systems.
In 2026, eight new road projects with a total length of nearly 286.9 km and a combined value of USD 334.9 million are scheduled to begin, including the rehabilitation of the Guliston–Kulob road (32.2 km, USD 80 million), Baljuvon–Sarikhosor road (58 km, USD 58 million), the Murghab–Kulma section (67 km, USD 78 million), construction of three bridges on the Labijar–Sangvor route (USD 17.1 million), construction of the Kulob bypass road (11 km, USD 30 million), rehabilitation of the Vahdat–Obigarm road (70 km, USD 28.8 million), construction of the Khistevarz–Konibodom (Arka) road (9.7 km, USD 4.5 million), and the Khorugh–Roshtqala road (39 km, USD 38.5 million).
The Tajik–Afghan Friendship Bridge, built by the United States in 2004, remains an important link connecting Tajikistan to Afghanistan and South Asia.
Tajikistan faces significant challenges in maintaining its road infrastructure. According to the Asian Transport Observatory, the country faces estimated average annual losses of $14.5 million to transport infrastructure, with 91% of these losses concentrated in the road sector.

### Rail

The railroad system consists of approximately 680 kilometres of 1,520 mm (4 ft 11+27⁄32 in) broad gauge, non-electrified single-track lines. According to Tajikistan's Ministry of Transport, the total length exceeds 980 kilometres, including approach and technical tracks. In the first half of 2025, rail accounted for approximately 70% of international freight traffic. In 2025, railway transport (including loading, dispatch, and transit shipments) carried 7.4 million tons of cargo, which is 10.9 percent more than in 2024. The network consists of three disconnected segments (northern, central, and southern), which require transit through Uzbekistan and Turkmenistan for interconnection.
The principal segments are in the southern region, connecting the capital with the industrial areas of the Hisor and Vakhsh valleys, and with Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, Kazakhstan, and Russia. The Bokhtar–Kulob railway connects the Kulob District with the central area of the country. The Vahdat–Yavan railway (48.65 km) is another important line, connecting the Districts of Republican Subordination with Khatlon region.
Railway development in 2025 centered on electrification plans and regional integration. Tajik Railways began preliminary work on electrifying the 62-kilometer Vahdat–Pakhtaobod line, which links eastern Dushanbe with the Uzbek border. This section of the line serves a commuter train that runs daily between Dushanbe-1 station and Pakhtaobod, with 15 intermediate stops. The travel time is approximately one hour. Authorities also explored electrification of the Kanibadam–Bekabad route. Feasibility studies continued for a proposed 51-kilometer railway connecting Jaloliddini Balkhi with Nizhniy Pyandzh, discussed within cooperation frameworks involving South Korea. Tajikistan has also been examining possible participation in the China–Kyrgyzstan–Uzbekistan railway project, which is expected to span more than 530 kilometers and include 20 stations.
Iran and Tajikistan have commenced a new chapter aimed at enhancing railway cooperation through the signing of a memorandum of understanding (MoU), seeking to strengthen and expand railway trade. In July 2025, Afghanistan, Uzbekistan, and Pakistan signed an agreement to begin a feasibility study for the "Afghan-Trans" railway project.

### Air

Tajikistan had 26 airports in 2009, of which 18 had paved runways, with two having runways longer than 3,000 metres. In 2025, Tajikistan's international airports served 2,963,000 passengers, marking a 21% increase compared to 2024. Major airports include:

Dushanbe International Airport – the country's main hub, featuring a 3,100-metre main runway capable of accommodating Boeing 737s and Airbus A320s. In 2025, construction began on a new 1,500-metre taxiway and 10 additional parking lots for large aircraft as part of an "Open Skies" policy. The "Open Skies" policy, introduced in June 2025, abolished the previous principle of flight parity, which limited the number of flights operated by foreign airlines, equating them to the number of flights operated by domestic carriers. A new terminal is also under construction at Dushanbe International Airport, with Turkish specialists preparing the design documentation and the facility scheduled to open within one to two years. In December 2025, President Rahmon ordered the renovation of the international airports of Dushanbe, Bokhtar, and Kulob to bring them into line with modern service standards.
Khujand Airport – features a single runway measuring 3,185 metres in length and 50 metres in width.
Kulob Airport – features a single asphalt runway measuring 3,000 metres in length and 42 metres in width.

### Electric vehicles and green mobility

Tajikistan has seen a dramatic increase in electric vehicle (EV) adoption. As of June 2025, the number of registered EVs exceeded 34,000 units, representing more than 5% of the country's total vehicle fleet. This shift has contributed to a reduction of 18,500 tons in liquefied gas imports in the first half of 2025. Since April 2025, Tajikistan has exempted new EVs (less than one year old) from import customs duties.

### Urban transport

Urban transport modernization gained momentum in 2025, particularly in the capital Dushanbe. Tajikistan announced plans to begin work on a light metro system in Dushanbe, with a feasibility study scheduled for completion in 2025 and construction planned between 2028 and 2033. The first underground line will span 10.5 kilometres, linking the southern gate of the capital (Darvozai) with central areas.
The EBRD is supporting the reconstruction of Dushanbe's road infrastructure through a newly established Road Reconstruction Project Implementation Group under the Ministry of Transport. Tajikistan and China have also agreed to step up work on the construction of two-level road interchanges in Dushanbe.
As of September 2025, Dushanbe's public transport fleet increased by 20%, reaching 380 buses, 65 trolleybuses, and over 1,100 minibuses. Additionally, 60 electric buses operate on 8 city routes, with plans to increase this number to 90 units. Authorities had planned a full transition of Dushanbe's taxi fleet to electric vehicles by September 2025, though while significant progress was made, the deadline was not fully met.
In the Sughd region, a new 12.7-kilometre section of the Dehmoy–Konibodom road is planned at a cost of over $8 million, with 11 road bridges and 17 bus stops already constructed along the route.

### Regional and international cooperation

Tajikistan has been actively pursuing regional transport integration. In July 2026, Iran, Afghanistan, and Tajikistan signed a trilateral transit corridor agreement in Mashhad, establishing a joint road transit corridor aimed at boosting regional trade. Under the agreement, a new transit route connecting the three countries will be activated, with the first trial cargo shipment expected within one month. The agreement also includes the formation of a joint working group tasked with addressing administrative obstacles, facilitating visas for drivers, reducing border delays and developing shared insurance mechanisms.
China has become a significant economic partner under the Belt and Road Initiative, with total Chinese investment in Tajikistan reaching approximately $4.1 billion between 2007 and 2023. Over 700 Chinese companies operate in the country, spanning infrastructure, mining, energy, and manufacturing sectors. Following the signing of a friendship treaty between China and Tajikistan in May, approximately $8 billion in economic and infrastructure agreements were signed, expanding cooperation in transport, infrastructure and security.
Tajikistan and Kazakhstan are also expanding transport cooperation, with road freight transportation between the two countries increasing during the first four months of 2026, with total cargo volumes rising 5.8% and the number of vehicles increasing 17.9% compared with the same period last year.

### Challenges and vulnerabilities

Tajikistan's transport sector faces several significant challenges. According to the Asian Transport Observatory, the country faces estimated average annual losses of $14.5 million to transport infrastructure, with 91% of these losses concentrated in the road sector. Greenhouse gas emissions from road transport are growing at an annual rate of 17.9%.
Security concerns have impacted infrastructure projects, as demonstrated by the suspension of the Dushanbe–Kulma Highway project following attacks on Chinese nationals. Out of 225 registered transport companies, only 107 have their own material and technical base, with the rest operating through leasing arrangements, affecting service quality.
Traffic congestion in Dushanbe remains a major concern, with the Minister of Transport ordering urgent measures in October 2025, including the completion of a comprehensive road infrastructure inventory, the development of a digital road map, the use of digital technology and artificial intelligence, and the transition to cashless payment systems across all transport services by the end of 2025. Proposed solutions include restrictions on personal vehicle use, congestion charges on central streets, installation of smart traffic lights, development of paid parking, construction of multi-storey parking facilities, stricter regulations on taxis, and dedicated bus lanes.
The transport sector's budget allocation has been reduced by 24% in the 2025–2027 program, potentially limiting the pace of infrastructure development. Additionally, the Istiklol Tunnel project faced suspension due to a $2.16 million funding shortfall from Iran.
Despite these challenges, Tajikistan continues to pursue an ambitious transport modernization agenda, with multiple international partners supporting road rehabilitation, railway electrification, airport upgrades, and the development of sustainable urban transport solutions.

In 2023, Tajikistan was estimated to have a population of 10,389,799, according to World Bank data. The Tajiks, who speak Tajik, are the main ethnic group, while there are minorities of Uzbeks and Russians, whose numbers are declining owing to emigration. This makes Tajikistan the only country in Central Asia to have a minority of Turkic people and a majority of Iranic people. The Pamiris of Badakhshan, the Yaghnobi people, and a minority of Ismailis are considered to belong to the larger group of Tajiks. Citizens of Tajikistan are called Tajikistanis.

In 1989, ethnic Russians in Tajikistan made up 7.6% of the population; by 1998, the proportion had fallen to approximately 0.5% following the Tajikistani Civil War, which had displaced the majority of ethnic Russians. After the end of the war, Russian emigration continued. The ethnic German population of Tajikistan has also declined owing to emigration: having peaked at 38,853 in 1979, it has "almost vanished" since the collapse of the Soviet Union.
The Tajiks are the principal ethnic group in most of Tajikistan and in northern and western Afghanistan; there are more Tajiks in Afghanistan than in Tajikistan. Tajiks are a minority in Uzbekistan. Approximately 2.4 million Tajik citizens were officially registered in Russia in 2021.

Tajikistan is a secular state with a constitution providing for freedom of religion, but nevertheless it heavily regulates the practices of its Muslim majority. Sunni Islam of the Hanafi school has been officially recognised by the government since 2009. The government has declared two Islamic holidays, Eid ul-Fitr and Eid al-Adha, as state holidays. According to a US State Department release and Pew research group, the population of Tajikistan is 98% Muslim. Approximately 87–95% of them are Sunni and roughly 3% are Shia and roughly 7% are non-denominational Muslims. The Shia part of the population predominantly live in the Gorno-Badakhshan Autonomous Region and are followers of the Ismaili branch of Shia Islam. The remaining 2% of the population are followers of Russian Orthodoxy, Protestantism, Zoroastrianism and Buddhism. Muslims fast during Ramadan, while about one third in the countryside and 10% in the cities observe daily prayer and dietary restrictions.
As of January 2016, as part of an "anti-radicalisation campaign", police in the Khatlon region reportedly shaved the beards of 13,000 men and shut down 160 shops selling the hijab. Shaving beards and discouraging women from wearing hijabs is part of a government campaign targeting trends that are deemed "alien and inconsistent with Tajik culture", and "to preserve secular traditions".
There is a concern for religious institutions becoming active in the political sphere. The Islamic Renaissance Party (IRP), a combatant in the 1992–1997 Civil War and then-proponent of the creation of an Islamic state in Tajikistan, constitutes no more than 30% of the government by statute. Membership in Hizb ut-Tahrir, a militant Islamic party which aims for an overthrow of secular governments and the unification of Tajiks under one Islamic state, is illegal and members are subject to arrest and imprisonment.
By law, religious communities must register with the State Committee on Religious Affairs (SCRA) and with local authorities. Registration with the SCRA requires a charter, a list of ten or more members, and evidence of local government approval for the prayer site location. Religious groups that do not have a physical structure are not allowed to gather publicly for prayer. Failure to register can result in fines and closure of a place of worship. There are reports that registration at the local level is sometimes difficult to obtain. People under the age of 18 are barred from public religious practice.
Approximately 1.6% of the population in Tajikistan is Christian, mostly Orthodox Christians. The territory of Tajikistan is part of the Dushanbe and Tajikistan Diocese of the Central Asian Metropolitan District of the Russian Orthodox Moscow Patriarchate. The country is home to communities of Catholics, Armenian Christians, Protestants, Lutherans, Jehovah's Witnesses, Baptists, Mormons, and Adventists.
Bukharan Jews have lived in Tajikistan since the 2nd century BC. In the 1940s, the Jewish community of Tajikistan numbered nearly 30,000 people. Most were Persian‑speaking Bukharan Jews who had lived in the region for millennia, along with Ashkenazi Jews from Eastern Europe who resettled there in the Soviet era. As of 2011, the Jewish population was estimated at fewer than 500, with roughly half living in Dushanbe.

### Health

The state's Ministry of Labour and Social Welfare reported that 104,272 disabled people were registered in Tajikistan in 2000. The government of Tajikistan and the World Bank considered activities to support this part of the population, as described in the World Bank's Poverty Reduction Strategy Paper. Public expenditure on health was 1% of GDP in 2004.
Life expectancy at birth was estimated at 69 years in 2020. The infant mortality rate was approximately 30.42 deaths per 1,000 live births in 2018. In 2014, there were 2.1 physicians per 1,000 people, higher than in any other low‑income country except North Korea.
Tajikistan has experienced a decrease in hospital beds per capita since 1992, following the dissolution of the USSR, although the number remains at 4.8 beds per 1,000 people, above the world average of 2.7.
According to the World Bank, 96% of births are attended by skilled health staff, up from 66.6% in 1999.
In 2010, the country experienced an outbreak of polio that caused more than 457 cases in both children and adults and resulted in 29 deaths, before being brought under control.
In the summer of 2021, the coronavirus ravaged the country, and the Tajik president's sister reportedly died of COVID-19 in a hospital. According to local media, the president's sister's sons physically assaulted the health minister and a senior doctor.
In 2023, according to the World Health Organization, Tajikistan received certification declaring it a malaria‑free country.

### Education

An estimated 99.8% of the population of Tajikistan have the ability to read and write.
Public education in Tajikistan consists of 11 years of primary and secondary education and the government planned to implement a 12-year system in 2016. There are a number of tertiary education institutions including Khujand State University, which has 76 departments in 15 faculties, Tajikistan State University of Law, Business, & Politics, Khorugh State University, Agricultural University of Tajikistan, Tajik National University, and other institutions. Universities were established during the Soviet Era. As of 2008 tertiary education enrollment was 17%, below the sub-regional average of 37%, while higher than any other low-income country after Syria. Tajiks left the education system due to "low" demand in the labour market for people with "extensive" educational training or professional skills.
Public spending on education was relatively constant between 2005–2012 and fluctuated from 3.5% to 4.1% of GDP below the OECD average of 6%. The United Nations reported that the level of spending was "severely inadequate to meet the requirements of the country's high-needs education system."
According to a UNICEF-supported survey, about 25% of girls in Tajikistan fail to complete compulsory primary education because of poverty and gender bias, while literacy is "generally high" in Tajikistan. Estimates of out of school children range from 4.6% to 19.4% with the majority being girls.
In September 2017, the University of Central Asia launched its second campus in Khorog, Tajikistan, offering majors in Earth & Environmental Sciences and Economics. Tajikistan was ranked 108th in the Global Innovation Index in 2025.
Science in the territory of Tajikistan achieved "success" in the Middle Ages, and scientific organisations were created in the Soviet period. During the period of independence, the scientific sphere has experienced a "crisis": the annual number of patent applications for inventions decreased in 1994–2011 from 193 to 5. A contribution to science is made by universities, where in 2011 6707 researchers worked, of which 2450 had academic degrees.

## Culture

The culture of Tajikistan has developed over several thousand years. Tajik culture can be divided into two areas, Metropolitan and Kuhiston (Highland). Modern city centres include Dushanbe (the capital), Khujand, Kulob, and Panjakent.
The Tajik language is the mother tongue of around 80% of the citizens of Tajikistan. There are Uzbek, Kyrgyz and Russian minorities. The Yaghnobi people live in areas of northern Tajikistan. The estimated number of Yaghnobis is about 25,000. Forced migrations in the 20th century decimated their numbers. They speak the Yaghnobi language, which is the only direct descendant of the Sogdian language and has often been called Neo-Sogdian in academic literature.
Tajikistan artisans created the Dushanbe Tea House, which was presented in 1988 as a gift to the sister city of Boulder, Colorado.
In the country, especially among women from the indigenous population, the wearing of traditional national clothing is preserved. The seamstresses and embroiderers of regions of Tajikistan use factory fabrics and local needlework embroidery for home decoration and women's clothing. The practice of Chakan embroidery is preserved among women in certain areas, passing the knowledge down from one generation to the next.

### Festivals and national holidays

Tajikistan celebrates several ancient national holidays that form the core of its cultural identity. These include Nowruz, Mehrgon, Sada, and Tirgon, which are recognized as the most valuable elements of Tajik intangible cultural heritage.
Nowruz is the most important holiday in Tajikistan, marking the spring equinox, the beginning of the solar year, and the New Year on the Iranian calendar. Nowruz, meaning "New Day," heralds the arrival of spring and is observed annually on March 21, the vernal equinox. The celebration traces back over 6,000 years, with historical ties to the Silk Road. In 2026, Dushanbe was selected as the main center for international Nowruz celebrations. The International Festival "Nowruz – Cultural and Global Heritage" is held annually during the third decade of March. The holiday is celebrated from 21 to 24 March, with government employees receiving a total of 10 days off.
Mehrgon is an ancient harvest festival rooted in Zoroastrian traditions, dedicated to the deity Mehr (Mithra). In the ancient Persian calendar, Mehrgon fell in mid-autumn, symbolizing the end of agricultural work and a time to thank the gods for prosperity. It was revived in 2009 by presidential decree and added to Tajikistan's national list of intangible cultural heritage in 2017.
Sada is one of the oldest holidays of the Tajik people, symbolizing the approach of Nowruz and the arrival of spring. According to scholars, Sada falls fifty nights and fifty days before Nowruz and serves as a reminder to farmers to prepare for the spring sowing. The name "Sada" is usually associated with the number one hundred, or fifty days and fifty nights, before Nowruz. In December 2023, the joint nomination of Tajikistan and Iran for the "Sada Festival" was inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity.
Tirgon is another ancient festival that, along with Sada, Navruz, and Mehrgon, is celebrated as part of Tajikistan's rich cultural heritage.

### Music and performing arts

Tajikistan has a rich musical heritage, with two genres – Shashmaqom and Falak – recognized as masterpieces of intangible cultural heritage.
Shashmaqom is the basis of Tajik traditional music and the highest manifestation of the musical mind of the people. Shashmaqom, meaning "six maqoms", constitutes a fusion of vocal and instrumental music, melodic and rhythmic idioms and poetry. The system of musical grammar of professional traditional Tajik music has been formed on the basis of maqoms. The six maqoms are Buzurg, Rost, Navo, Dugoh, Segoh, and Iroq. The poetic texts of the vocal sections are drawn from the works of classical Persian-Tajik poets such as Hafez, Saadi, and Jami. Traditional instruments accompanying Shashmaqom include the tanbur, dutar, and doira. The musical genre emerged in the pre-Islamic period in Bukhara and later spread throughout the Eastern world. In 2003, Shashmaqom was inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. May 12 is celebrated in Tajikistan as "Shashmaqom Day." In June 2025, a Shashmaqom cultural program was held in Paris to mark the 700th anniversary of Hafez Sherazi and the 60th anniversary of UNESCO's water sciences.
Falak is a musical genre consisting of a cycle of folk songs, accompanied by string musical instruments such as the dumbra, tablak, and ghizhak. Falak, meaning 'heaven', 'fortune' and 'universe', is the traditional folklore music of the mountain people of Tajikistan. The expressive and philosophical musical genre may be performed by a male or female soloist, either a cappella, or with a single instrumental accompaniment or an ensemble and dancers. It is performed in the Khatlon region, Badakhshan, and the Rasht valley. In October 2025, an international symposium titled "Falak and State Independence – from Revival to Global Recognition" was held in Dushanbe to comprehensively examine Falak as a unique phenomenon in Tajikistan's musical culture and its role in strengthening national identity.
Other traditional musical genres include Rubobnavozi (skill of playing the rubab), Qonunnavozi (skill of playing the qonun), and Amiri (folk songs and melodies performed at celebrations and concerts).

### Literature

Tajik literature has a rich history dating back over a thousand years. Rudaki (858–941 CE), known as the "Adam of Poets," is considered a father of classical Persian literature and takes centre stage in Tajikistan's literary heritage. He is regarded as the first major poet to write in New Persian. Although only a small portion of his work has survived, his influence on Persian and Tajik poetry remains profound.
Sadriddin Aini (1878–1954) is regarded as the founder of modern Tajik literature. He was a poet, writer, historian, translator, scholar, and political figure. Aini excelled in research and literary criticism, and his papers on the lives and works of Rudaki are particularly notable.
In 2025, Tajikistan participated in the 36th Tehran International Book Fair with more than 400 newly published titles. Works by classical poets such as Rudaki, Ferdowsi, Hafez, Saadi, Bidel, and Jami, as well as contemporary writers like Mirzo Tursunzoda, Layeq Shir-Ali, Mu'min Qanoat, Gulrukhsor, and Muhammad Ghoyib, were presented. The Persian translation of Sadriddin Aini's novel Odina was published in Iran in 2025. A collection of poems titled Kamandi Zulf by Zulfiya Atoi, a folk poet of Tajikistan, was published by the ECO Cultural Institute in Tehran.

### Visual arts and handicrafts

Chakan embroidery is a distinctive form of Tajik embroidery that was inscribed in 2018 on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. The art of Chakan embroidery involves sewing ornaments, images of flowers and symbolic drawings with colourful threads on cotton or silk fabrics. The National Festival "Sad Gul-i Chakan" was held on 5 March 2025 at the Dushanbe Philharmonic Hall, with craftswomen from Sughd, Khatlon, Badakhshan, and the Rasht valleys displaying their works.
Suzani embroidery is another important Tajik handicraft. Tajik masters embroidered suzani wall panels, zardevor ceiling friezes, borpush, kars, ruidjo covers and blankets, joynamaz prayer mats, as well as chakan dresses. The city of Panjakent was designated as a "World Craft City for Panjakent Suzani Embroidery" by the World Crafts Council on 21 October 2025.
In September 2025, the exhibition "The World of Tajik Embroidery" opened at the Azerbaijan National Carpet Museum, featuring 56 unique pieces from the Golden Treasury of the National Museum of Tajikistan. The exhibition included suzani embroidery, chakan dresses, popurduzi embroidery, blankets, prayer rugs, embroidered men's waistbands (miyonband), mirror covers (oinakhalta), wall embroidery, and tablecloths.

### Cuisine

Tajik cuisine is hearty, filling, and rooted in Central Asian and Persian influences.
Oshi Palav (pilaf) is the national dish in Tajikistan. It is a rice dish made with shredded yellow turnip or carrots, pieces of meat, all fried together in vegetable oil or mutton fat in a special qazan (a wok-shaped cauldron) over an open flame. There are up to 200 varieties of the dish. Oshi palav is prepared for regular mealtimes, as well as social gatherings, celebrations, and rituals. In 2016, Oshi Palav was inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity.
Qurutob is a traditional dish made from pieces of flatbread soaked in yoghurt or dried curd sauce, topped with onions and herbs. Other traditional dishes include mantu (steamed dumplings), shurbo and piti (meat and vegetable soups), laghman and ugro (noodle soups), and sambusa (stuffed pastries). Traditional meals typically begin with dried fruits, nuts, and halva, proceed to soups and meat dishes, and conclude with plov.

### Telecommunications

Tajikistan's communications market grew 13.7% in 2025 to 4.9 billion somoni.
Average mobile internet speed increased from 7.2 Mbps at the beginning of 2025 to 35.4 Mbps in 2026. Fixed internet speed increased from 30 Mbps to over 40 Mbps in 2025, with plans to reach 100 Mbps within two years. Average internet consumption per user has increased tenfold over the past five years, from 3 to 30 GB. Fixed internet prices have decreased by an average of 55%.
Tajikistan is in the early stages of 5G deployment. A memorandum on the establishment of a national 5G consortium was signed between the Communications Service and the country's mobile operators—Indigo Tajikistan, TT Mobile, Babilon M, Tacom, and Tojiktelecom—to coordinate the rollout of next-generation mobile technologies. Currently, 84 5G base stations are active.
In October 2025, a licensing agreement was signed with Starlink Tajikistan (SpaceX), enabling the launch of Starlink operations in the country.

### Cultural infrastructure

In 2025, 44 cultural facilities were built in Tajikistan, while 120 facilities—including libraries, cultural centres, museums, parks, and music schools—were fully renovated and reconstructed. The Ministry of Culture participated in 65 international scientific and cultural events across 21 countries.
The National Theatre of Tajikistan is under construction in Dushanbe, adjacent to the National Museum and the National Flag Park. The building will have 2 underground and 5 above-ground floors, reaching a height of 34.5 meters. A State Program for the Development of Performing Arts for 2025–2029 has been approved, with more than 14 million somoni allocated for theatre development.
The State Academic Opera and Ballet Theatre named after Sadriddin Aini signed a cooperation memorandum with the Karaganda Academic Musical Comedy Theatre (Kazakhstan) in September 2025, with plans for performances in Rome in 2026.

### Cinema

The second Dushanbe International Film Festival "Toji Somon" was held from 15 to 20 October 2025 under the motto "History of the Development of Tajiks." Filmmakers from 20 countries participated. The film Black Rabbit, White Rabbit directed by Shahram Mokri (Iran) was selected as Tajikistan's submission for the Best International Feature Film category at the Oscars.

### Sport

The national sport of Tajikistan is gushtigiri, a traditional form of wrestling. The sport has seen significant development in recent years. The first edition of a national team club competition was held on 5 October 2025, with the participation of 98 athletes from 14 sports clubs nationwide. According to the International Gushtingiri Federation's operational calendar, the World Club Championships are scheduled for December 2026. The Tajikistan Gushtingiri Federation was awarded as the "Best Non-Olympic Sports Federation".
The sixth international Gushtingiri tournament for the Dushanbe Mayor's Cup was held on 26 April 2025, at the Tennis Palace, bringing together wrestlers from more than 25 countries. The 2025 national championship was held on 22–23 February 2025 at the National University of Tajikistan, featuring 410 athletes competing across 7 men's and 5 women's weight categories. The 2026 national championship is scheduled for 10–11 February 2026 in Dushanbe. Dushanbe will host the second World Gushtingiri Championship on 15–16 August 2026, with a total prize fund of $400,000; winners in each weight category will receive $20,000, silver medalists $10,000, and bronze medalists $5,000.
Another traditional sport is buzkashi, a game played on horseback, similar to polo. Buzkashi may be played as an individual sport and as a team sport. The aim of the game is to grab a 50 kg dead goat, ride clear of the other players, get back to the starting point and drop it in a designated circle. It is played at Nowruz celebrations. In 2025, buzkashi was featured prominently during the Nowruz celebrations in Dushanbe.

Tajikistan has sent athletes to every Summer Olympic Games and four Winter Olympic Games since gaining independence and has enjoyed limited success. At the 2024 Summer Olympics in Paris, Tajikistan was represented by 14 athletes (11 men and 3 women) across 6 sports. The country secured three bronze medals, finishing 79th among 206 participating nations. The medalists were judokas Somon Mahmadbekov (up to 81 kg) and Temur Rakhimov (over 100 kg), and boxer Davlat Boltaev (up to 92 kg). Tajikistan's best results have come in the hammer throw, with Andrey Abduvaliyev and Dilshod Nazarov each winning gold in the men's event in 1992 and 2016 respectively (albeit with Abduvaliyev participating as part of the Unified Team). Tajikistan's highest medal haul at an Olympic Games came in the 2024 Summer Olympics, where its athletes won three medals. The country's total Olympic medal count as an independent nation now stands at 7 medals (1 gold, 1 silver, 5 bronze). Cash rewards were given to the medalists, with bronze medalists receiving $4,500 each.
At the 2025 Asian Winter Games in Harbin, China, Tajikistan was represented by four athletes (all male) in alpine skiing, though the country did not secure any medals. The Secretary-General of the National Olympic Committee emphasized that participation in the event was an important step for the development of winter sports.
At the 2025 Asian Youth Para Games in Dubai, the Tajikistani team secured 4 medals (1 gold, 3 silver). Ahmadi Fathiddin set a world record in weightlifting in the under-65 kg category with a lift of 135 kg, winning the gold medal.
The country is actively preparing for the 2026 Asian Games in Aichi-Nagoya, Japan, and the 2028 Summer Olympics. The Tajikistani team is expected to participate in 41 sports disciplines at the 2026 Asian Games.
The Tajikistan Football Federation is the governing body of football in Tajikistan. The men's national team made its historic debut at the 2023 AFC Asian Cup in Qatar, where it advanced to the quarter-finals. The team finished second in Group A behind hosts and eventual champions Qatar, ahead of China and Lebanon, before defeating the United Arab Emirates on penalties in the round of 16. The team participated in the AFC Challenge Cup on four occasions, winning the inaugural edition in 2006.
The men's team finished 2025 with a FIFA ranking of 101st. Among Asian Football Confederation members, the team ranks 17th. During 2025, the team played 10 official matches, recording six wins, two draws, and two losses. The team's next official match is scheduled for 31 March 2026 in Hisar.
The women's side has twice participated in the CAFA Women's Championship, finishing third in the 2018 edition and hosting the tournament in 2022.
The youth teams have also achieved success. The under-17 team won the CAFA U17 Championship in 2025 in Tashkent, and the under-18 team secured a bronze medal at the CAFA U20 Championship in 2025. The under-17 team also participated in the 2025 FIFA U-17 World Cup in Qatar with players from the FIFA Talent Academy.
The 2026 season of the Tajikistan Higher League began on 6 March 2026 with 12 teams. The league has implemented a foreign player quota of a maximum of 7 players, with only 5 allowed on the field simultaneously. Each team is also required to include at least one player born in 2008 or later in the starting lineup. The Super Cup is scheduled for 23 May 2026 at the Hisar Central Stadium, and the Cup final will be held at the Vakhsh Arena in Bokhtar. A women's team will become mandatory for all clubs from 2027.
A new National Stadium of Tajikistan is under construction in Dushanbe on a 9.5-hectare site with a capacity of more than 30,000 seats. The stadium will feature 45 office rooms and a conference hall, 34 high-level VIP viewing areas, 9 medical facilities, 36 canteens, and a Tajik football museum built to international standards. A fire broke out at the construction site in August 2025, but the project continues. The Vasi' Stadium in the Vasi' district of Khatlon region was opened on 22 October 2025 with a capacity of 21,000 seats, featuring a 105×68 meter natural grass football pitch and an athletics track. The construction cost approximately 69 million somoni and includes two mini-football fields, volleyball and basketball courts. It is part of the 2022–2026 football development program, which aims to build 130 sports projects in Khatlon province.
Several other sports are also actively pursued. The Tajikistan Cricket Federation was formed in 2012 as the governing body for the sport of cricket in Tajikistan. It was granted affiliate membership of the Asian Cricket Council in the same year. At the CAFA Nations Cup 2025, Tajikistan is placed in Group B alongside India, Afghanistan, and Iran.
In 2008, rugby union was officially registered with the Ministry of Justice, and there are three men's clubs. In November 2025, Asia Rugby held a meeting with the Minister of Sports and the National Olympic Committee of Tajikistan with the aim of building a solid foundation for the Rugby Federation of Tajikistan through technical training, educational programs, and financial assistance.
Khorugh, capital of Gorno-Badakhshan Autonomous Region, is the location of highest altitude where bandy has been played.
Tajikistan has one ski resort, called Safed Dara (formerly Takob), near the town of Varzob. Safed Dara, meaning "White Valley" in Tajik, is located approximately 50 kilometers from Dushanbe, about an hour's drive. The resort sits among the snowy peaks of Tajikistan at an elevation reaching up to 3,020 meters. In 2025, the resort added an additional 1.5 km gondola run, doubling the size of the skiable terrain. The resort features beginner-friendly slopes, a few lifts, and untouched mountain terrain for more adventurous skiers and snowboarders. A one-day tour package is available for $180 per person, offering skiing, snowboarding, snowtubing, and cable car rides over 8 to 10 hours.
The Tajikistan national volleyball team hosted the CAVA U16 Girls' Volleyball Championship in July 2025 at the Olympic Palace in Dushanbe, marking the country's first international volleyball event. In November 2025, the women's national team lost to Iran 3-0 at the Islamic Solidarity Games.
In tennis, the 15th edition of the President's Cup of Tajikistan was held in September 2025 with the participation of more than 100 athletes. The J30 Dushanbe 2026 tournament is registered on the ITF calendar for June 2026.
In badminton, Tajikistani athletes won bronze medals in the doubles under-15 category at the Central Asian Badminton Championships in Tashkent in September 2025.
In kurash, the Tajikistan national team won 8 medals across various age categories at the Asian Kurash Championships in South Korea in August 2025.
In boxing, Behruz Umarov won Tajikistan's first gold medal at the 2025 CIS Games in Azerbaijan in October 2025. The Tajikistan national boxing championship was held in Khujand in October 2025 with the participation of 112 male and 19 female boxers. The national boxing team also participated in the 2025 World Championships in Dubai in December 2025.
In judo, Tajikistan hosted the Dushanbe Grand Slam 2025 from 2–4 May 2025, one of the most important judo events in the world. Temur Rakhimov (+100 kg) won a bronze medal at the World Championships in Budapest in July 2025. In November–December 2025, Muhiddin Asadullaev (under 73 kg) won a silver medal and Sunnatullah Musavi (under 81 kg) won a bronze medal at the Abu Dhabi Grand Slam. Tajikistan also hosted the Asian Junior and Youth Cup in Dushanbe in 2025, and the men's national judo championship was held in Dushanbe in December 2025.
In powerlifting, the Tajikistan national team won 5 gold medals at the Asian Championships in Vietnam in July 2025. In December 2025, Davlatmand Adinaev won a gold medal at the WRPF World Championships in Moscow in the 110 kg category with a total of 735 kg.
In taekwondo, Tajikistani athletes won 2 bronze medals (in the junior and youth divisions) at the Asian Championships in Koching, Malaysia in July 2025. In September 2025, Abduljabbor Bahador (under 54 kg) and Nurullah Muradov (under 74 kg) won bronze medals at the CIS Games 2025.
In karate, Tajikistani athletes won 32 medals (13 gold, 12 silver, 7 bronze) at the WFKS-2025 World Cup in Moscow in April 2025.
In wushu, the Tajikistan national team won 19 medals at the World Wushu-Kungfu Championships in China in November 2025.
In para sports, an international seminar on "Mastering Advanced Techniques in Wheelchair Basketball" was held in Dushanbe in October 2025 with the support of TİKA.
Esports is an emerging sector in Tajikistan. The President's Cup esports tournament was held in November 2025, featuring competitions in Counter-Strike 2, Dota 2, PUBG M, Mobile Legends: Bang Bang, and FC 25. The first intercompany esports tournament in Dushanbe was held in June 2025, with teams from Dushanbe City Bank, Humo Lab, MegaFon Tajikistan, and Accelerate Prosperity participating. The esports market in Tajikistan is steadily growing, with increasing interest from both players and spectators. A strategic partnership between Tcell and Esports Championship Services (ESCS) was announced to launch a new era of interactive gaming in Tajikistan. Tajikistan also participates in the CIS Youth Esports Games.
In total, over 2024–2025, Tajikistani athletes won 958 medals (274 gold, 268 silver, 406 bronze) in international competitions.
Tajikistan has signed sports cooperation agreements with several countries. In March 2025, Tajikistan and Kyrgyzstan signed a 2025–2027 cooperation program in the field of sports. In April 2025, Tajikistan and Iran signed a sports cooperation agreement. Tajikistan also cooperates with China in martial arts (wushu).
The government has prioritized sports as a social policy, allocating 1,787,500 somoni (approximately $164,000) from the presidential reserve fund for bonuses to medalists of international competitions. Companies such as Parimatch Tajikistan, MegaFon Tajikistan, and Tojiktelecom sponsor the football league and national teams.
Despite progress, Tajikistan faces challenges in sports development. The country has limited winter sports development, participating only in alpine skiing. Doping remains a concern; in 2025, Lazizbek Mullajanov (an Olympic champion from Uzbekistan) was suspended for doping, which also affected Davlat Boltaev, who shared training sessions with him. The government has established a National Anti-Doping Center to address this issue. Despite infrastructure improvements, further investment in sports facilities is needed.
