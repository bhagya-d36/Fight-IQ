# UFC History

## How did the UFC start?

The UFC held its first event, UFC 1, on November 12, 1993, in Denver,
Colorado. It was originally created to find out which martial art was most
effective in a real fight, pitting practitioners of different disciplines
(boxing, wrestling, karate, Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu, sumo, and more) against each
other with very few rules.

## Who won UFC 1?

Royce Gracie, a Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu practitioner, won the UFC 1 tournament,
submitting larger and stronger opponents and proving the effectiveness of
ground grappling. His performance helped popularize BJJ worldwide.

## How did the rules evolve?

Early UFC events had almost no rules, banning only eye gouging and biting,
and had no weight classes, judges, or time limits in some fights. Due to
political pressure and being banned in many U.S. states during the late
1990s, the sport adopted the Unified Rules of Mixed Martial Arts, developed
with state athletic commissions like New Jersey and Nevada in the early
2000s. These rules introduced weight classes, timed rounds, a list of fouls,
and standardized judging.

## Who bought the UFC and when?

Zuffa LLC, led by Frank and Lorenzo Fertitta with Dana White as president,
bought the struggling UFC in 2001 and invested heavily in getting it
regulated and mainstream. In 2016, Zuffa sold the UFC to WME-IMG (later
Endeavor) for around $4 billion, one of the largest sports transactions in
history at the time. In 2023, Endeavor merged the UFC with WWE to form the
publicly traded TKO Group Holdings.

## What was "The Ultimate Fighter" and why did it matter?

"The Ultimate Fighter" (TUF) was a reality TV competition series that debuted
in 2005, putting aspiring fighters through a tournament while living together
under one roof. Its finale, decided by a fight between Forrest Griffin and
Stephan Bonnar, is widely credited with turning the UFC's popularity around
in the United States.

## What was the "golden era" of UFC growth?

Through the late 2000s and 2010s, the UFC's popularity grew rapidly behind
stars like Georges St-Pierre, Anderson Silva, Ronda Rousey (who led the
introduction of a women's division starting in 2013), and later Conor
McGregor, whose crossover stardom and pay-per-view success brought the
promotion to a new level of mainstream attention.
